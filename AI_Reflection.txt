AI_Reflection.txt

For this project I used Claude as my main AI tool.

How I started
I started by creating my own action plan and choosing technologies I had already worked with — either in my personal projects or at university. Only after that did I consult my plan with Claude, who helped me verify whether my technology choices were optimal and whether I had missed any steps. I then moved on to setting up the environment — this is where AI saved me a lot of time, because whenever I had a question or doubt I could get an answer right away.

How I used AI while writing code
I understand all the code I wrote — I know what happens where and why. When bugs appeared, I always tried to fix them myself first, looking for solutions online. AI helped me understand errors I couldn't figure out on my own. Most of the time I managed to fix things myself, but a few times I had to ask Claude Code to analyze the project, explain what was going wrong and help me fix it.

AI also helped me check whether my application plan covered all the requirements from the task — so I wouldn't miss any point by accident.

How I verified the code from AI
I always tried to understand the code I received from AI — I analyzed the connections between files so I knew what to expect. I kept an eye on AI at every step — when it suggested changes or optimizations, I didn't accept them without understanding why they were better.

A good example of verification: when designing the ERD, AI suggested creating a separate `seasons` table. I questioned this — a season is just one integer, a separate table would be over-engineering with no real value. Instead I kept `season_year` as a simple INTEGER field in the `events` table. That was my decision, not AI's.

AI also suggested a few solutions that didn't follow 3NF — for example storing match results directly in the `events` table. I had to push back and convince AI to go with the correct approach — a separate `results` table. This shows I wasn't just blindly copying, I was thinking for myself.

Frontend and tests
I also used AI to help me create the basic styling and layout of the HTML pages. I'm not a frontend developer, so I asked Claude to suggest a simple, clean design for the event list, detail view and form. I understood what the CSS did and adjusted it where needed — for example the color scheme and table layout.

For the unit tests I used AI as well. I used Mockito for service tests and MockMvc for controller tests. AI helped me understand how mocking works — instead of a real database, Mockito creates fake objects that simulate repositories. This way the 11 tests run without Docker or PostgreSQL being active. Through this I also learned the difference between unit tests and integration tests.

What I learned
I learned a lot of valuable things through this project that I'm sure will pay off in the future.

One concept that was new to me was the N+1 problem in Hibernate. Through this project I understood exactly why it happens and how to solve it using JOIN FETCH. This was one of those moments where AI didn't just give me a solution but also explained why it's better — and that stuck with me.

I also learned that it's not worth only thinking about the data you have right now. Even when building a small system, it's worth thinking about the future — so it can handle larger and external data, so it's flexible. An example from this project: the `goals` and `cards` tables are empty in the provided dataset, but I designed them properly so they're ready for real data in the future.

This project also made me realize how important it is to design a database properly from the start — the decisions made at the design stage have a huge impact on the quality of the whole system.

I tried to understand every technical decision in this project — why Liquibase instead of ddl-auto, why JOIN FETCH, why a separate results table, why an interface in the service layer. Not everything was obvious to me right away, but I kept asking questions until I understood — and that's the approach I want to keep going forward.

My thoughts on AI as a tool
AI is a great tool for a developer — it speeds up work, helps find bugs that a person would spend much longer looking for, and makes the work more productive. A developer can focus more on building a well-thought-out solution rather than just writing code.

That said, AI is just a tool — you can't use it to build everything without understanding what it produced. AI also makes mistakes, repeats itself, and sometimes suggests solutions that aren't optimal. That's why I always verified what I received and tried to understand every line of code. I think that's what separates a developer who uses AI wisely from someone who just copies and pastes.
